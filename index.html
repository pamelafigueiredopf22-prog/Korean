한글 Hangul

The Korean alphabet. Tap any letter to hear it.

Korean isn't drawn as a line of letters — sounds stack into little square syllable blocks. A block is a consonant + a vowel: ㄱ + ㅏ = 가 (ga). Learn the pieces and you can read the blocks.

Vowels · 모음

These set the main sound of a block.

Combined vowels · 겹모음

Basic vowels run together into one sound. A few have nearly merged in modern Korean — ㅐ and ㅔ are both about e, and ㅙ ㅚ ㅞ all land near we.

Consonants · 자음

These start (and sometimes end) a block.

Tense consonants · 쌍자음

The same shapes doubled. Say them harder and tighter, with no puff of air — almost like you catch the sound before it leaves.

ㅇ is the quiet one. At the start of a block it makes no sound — it's just a placeholder so a vowel can stand alone: 아 is simply a. At the end of a block it sounds like ng.

문장 Sentence structure

Korean word order — and why the verb waits until the end.

English runs Subject → Verb → Object: “I drink coffee.” Korean runs Subject → Object → Verb — the verb always comes last.

저는 커피를 마셔요.
I · coffee · drink

The particles you already know (은/는, 이/가, 을/를) tag each word’s job, so Korean can reshuffle for emphasis. But the neutral, safe order is S–O–V, and the verb stays at the end no matter what.

과거 Past tense

A diary is mostly “today I did…”. Here’s how to say it.

Drop 다 from the dictionary form, look at the stem’s last vowel, and add one of three endings:

stem vowel ㅏ or ㅗ → 았어요
any other vowel → 었어요
하다 verbs → 했어요

Often the stem and ending squeeze together: 가 + 았어요 → 갔어요, 보 + 았어요 → 봤어요, 마시 + 었어요 → 마셨어요.
