한글 Hangul

The Korean alphabet. Tap any letter to hear it.

Korean isn't drawn as a line of letters — sounds stack into little square syllable blocks. A block is a consonant + a vowel: ㄱ + ㅏ = 가 (ga). Learn the pieces and you can read the blocks.

Vowels · 모음

These set the main sound of a block.

Combined vowels · 겹모음

Basic vowels run together into one sound. A few have nearly merged in modern Korean — ㅐ and ㅔ are both about e, and ㅙ ㅚ ㅞ all land near we.

Consonants · 자음

These start (and sometimes end) a block.

Tense consonants · 쌍자음

The same shapes doubled. Say them harder and tighter, with no puff of air — almost like you catch the sound before it leaves.

ㅇ is the quiet one. At the start of a block it makes no sound — it's just a placeholder so a vowel can stand alone: 아 is simply a. At the end of a block it sounds like ng.

발음 Pronunciation

Why Korean words aren’t said the way they’re spelled. Tap any example to hear it.

Korean spelling keeps each word looking the same, but speech runs syllables together, and sounds bend to fit their neighbours. These aren’t sloppiness or slang — they’re the standard rules (표준 발음법), and they’re what stands between knowing Hangul and actually reading Korean aloud.

Don’t memorise these. Read them once, then drill — they sink in through your ear, not your notes.
연음Linking

The big one. A final consonant (받침) can’t sit still if the next block starts with the silent ㅇ — it slides over into that empty slot.

받침The seven final sounds

Sixteen consonants can be written at the bottom of a block, but only seven sounds can actually close one: ㄱ ㄴ ㄷ ㄹ ㅁ ㅂ ㅇ. Everything else collapses into the nearest of those — ㅋ ㄲ → [ㄱ], ㅍ → [ㅂ], and ㄷ ㅌ ㅅ ㅆ ㅈ ㅊ ㅎ all → [ㄷ]. The sound also stops dead, with no puff of air after it.

비음화Nasalising

ㄴ and ㅁ are nasal, and they’re contagious. A hard stop landing in front of one turns nasal too: [ㄱ] → ㅇ, [ㄷ] → ㄴ, [ㅂ] → ㅁ.

유음화Liquidising

When ㄴ and ㄹ end up next to each other — in either order — the ㄴ gives in and becomes ㄹ.

경음화Tensing

After a [ㄱ] [ㄷ] [ㅂ] sound, a following ㄱ ㄷ ㅂ ㅅ ㅈ goes tense — ㄲ ㄸ ㅃ ㅆ ㅉ. Nothing is written differently; you just hear it tighten.

격음화Aspirating

ㅎ next to ㄱ ㄷ ㅂ ㅈ — before or after — merges with it and makes it breathy: ㅋ ㅌ ㅍ ㅊ.

ㅎ 탈락ㅎ dropping

The flip side: a ㅎ 받침 followed by a vowel just disappears. This one catches everybody — 좋아요 is never “조하요”.

구개음화Palatalising

ㄷ and ㅌ followed by 이 slide forward into ㅈ and ㅊ — the same laziness that turns English “would you” into “wouldja”.

겹받침Double batchim

Some blocks carry two consonants underneath (ㄺ ㅄ ㄵ ㄶ). Before a consonant only one of them sounds — but before a vowel, the second one links across instead: 읽다 [익따] yet 읽어요 [일거요].

조사 Particles

Little tags that tell you what job each word is doing.

English uses word order to show who did what to whom: “the cat drinks water” means something different from “the water drinks cat”. Korean tags each word instead — so the tag, not the position, carries the meaning. Three pairs matter first.

은 / 는Topic

Sets up what the sentence is about — “as for X…”. Use it to introduce yourself or your subject, and to contrast one thing with another.

저는 학생이에요.
As for me, I’m a student.
이 / 가Subject

Marks who or what is actually doing the verb, or being described. It tends to point something out as new or specific — “it’s the cat that’s cute”.

고양이가 귀여워요.
The cat is cute.
을 / 를Object

Marks what the verb is done to. This one is the least ambiguous of the three — if something is being drunk, eaten, read or drawn, it takes 을/를.

커피를 마셔요.
I drink coffee.

Then: which of the pair?

Once you know the job, the form is a spelling rule, not a judgement call. Look at the last letter of the noun:

ends in a consonant → 은 · 이 · 을
ends in a vowel → 는 · 가 · 를
It’s purely for the mouth: 이름은 is easier to say than 이름는.

The honest bit

Topic vs subject (은/는 vs 이/가) is the one that takes ages, and native speakers choose by nuance rather than rule — both are often grammatical in the same sentence. Don’t expect to nail it from a chart; you’ll absorb it from reading. That’s why every card in the drill tells you the role — so you can practise the ending rule cleanly, without guessing the nuance too.

문장 Sentence structure

Korean word order — and why the verb waits until the end.

English runs Subject → Verb → Object: “I drink coffee.” Korean runs Subject → Object → Verb — the verb always comes last.

저는 커피를 마셔요.
I · coffee · drink

The particles you already know (은/는, 이/가, 을/를) tag each word’s job, so Korean can reshuffle for emphasis. But the neutral, safe order is S–O–V, and the verb stays at the end no matter what.

과거 Past tense

A diary is mostly “today I did…”. Here’s how to say it.

Drop 다 from the dictionary form, look at the stem’s last vowel, and add one of three endings:

stem vowel ㅏ or ㅗ → 았어요
any other vowel → 었어요
하다 verbs → 했어요

Often the stem and ending squeeze together: 가 + 았어요 → 갔어요, 보 + 았어요 → 봤어요, 마시 + 었어요 → 마셨어요.

시제 Present & future

What you do, and what you’re going to do.

현재Present

Good news — it’s the same vowel rule you already learned for the past, just without the 았/었 in the middle. Drop 다, look at the stem’s last vowel:

stem vowel ㅏ or ㅗ → 아요
any other vowel → 어요
하다 verbs → 해요

This form covers both “I drink coffee” and “I’m drinking coffee” — Korean doesn’t split those. It also handles habits: 항상 커피를 마셔요.

미래Future

One pattern: -(으)ㄹ 거예요. Which half you use depends on the stem’s last sound — the same consonant-vs-vowel split as particles.

stem ends in a vowel → -ㄹ 거예요  가 → 갈 거예요
stem ends in a consonant → -을 거예요  먹 → 먹을 거예요

Three things worth getting right from the start:

  • It’s spelled 거예요, never 거에요 — even Koreans slip on this one.
  • There’s a space before it: 갈 거예요, not 갈거예요.
  • You’ll hear [갈 꺼예요] — that’s the tensing rule from Pronunciation. Don’t write the ㄲ.
All three tenses
어제 커피를 마셨어요.
오늘 커피를 마셔요.
내일 커피를 마실 거예요.
Yesterday I drank coffee. Today I drink coffee. Tomorrow I’ll drink coffee.
